Research Experience

In the first semester of my junior year, **I collaborated with Carlos Gustavo Moreira, a fellow of the Brazilian Academy of Sciences, and Dr. Zhang Yiwei from the Southern University of Science and Technology. We co-authored the paper "Graphs of Non-affine Continuous Function are Never Self-similar," exploring the self-similarity of continuous non-affine function graphs.** We proved that on a bounded closed set, if a continuous function is not self-affine, then its graph cannot be self-similar. This leads to the direct corollary that the graph of the highly self-similar Weierstrass function is not a self-similar set. The paper is currently under peer review for publication in the Proceedings of the American Mathematical Society. The application of Vitali covering techniques was crucial in our proof process, which highlighted to me the value of solidifying foundational knowledge in academic research and the importance of creativity in academia, achieved by diligently studying and internalizing the various techniques introduced in foundational courses, rather than stopping at superficial understanding. During this collaboration, I proposed the first step of the three-step proof and was responsible for writing the entire paper. This research experience has made me realize the decisive role of persistent thinking and proactive, open discussion in mathematical research. It has solidified my foundation in rigorous mathematical writing, and the grueling process of writing and refining the paper has also tempered my resolve, embracing the philosophy that good things come to those who wait.

Training Mathematical Intuition

During the functional analysis seminar I organized this winter break, after a classmate finished explaining the proof of the theorem that weak\* topology can be metrized, I habitually asked a question I often pose to myself: What essentially is this proof doing? After a moment of thought, I then asked a question that delves deeper into the essence: What exactly does the pairing of an element in a Banach space and a functional measure? Shortly after, I recalled an idea mentioned by this student in a previous discussion: an element of the dual space should represent a direction in the Banach space. I realized that every functional has its own null kernel, and the inner product (after some technical adjustments) quantitatively measures the deviation of the represented direction from the null kernel subspace. This is the essence of the step in the proof of the metrizability theorem for separable weak\* topological spaces that involves collecting countably many inner products: gathering all representative directions.

This unconventional method of thinking training, stepping out of technical details and asking oneself: What fundamentally is the mechanism that naturally gives rise to these phenomena? Such exploration sometimes allows my intuition to reach the destination before logic and technique do. This sense of mathematics has built my confidence, making me believe that I will be able to make discoveries and reach new insights in the vast field of mathematics in the future. 数学感觉的训练

在今年寒假我组织的泛函分析讨论班，在同学讲完弱星拓扑可度量化定理的证明之后，我习惯性地问出了一个我常常问自己的问题：这个证明它本质上做了一件什么事情？片刻的思考之后，我又问：所以一个巴拿赫空间的元素和一个泛函的配对内积到底衡量了什么？又片刻，我忆起这个同学在先前讨论班中提到的一个想法：对偶空间的一个元素应该会对应巴拿赫空间的一些方向。我意识到，每个泛函都有自己的消失核，而内积（经过一些技术修正）定量地衡量了代表的方向和消失核子空间的偏离程度，这也是可分弱星拓扑空间可度量化定理证明中收集可数多个内积步骤的实质：收集具有代表性的所有方向。

这样非传统的思维训练，即跳出技术细节，叩问自己：实质上是什么机理让这些现象自然生长出来？这样的探索使我有时能够让直觉先于逻辑和技术触及那个想去的地方。这种对数学的感觉建立了我的信心，让我相信自己将来能在数学的旷野里有所收获有所抵达。
